01The Challenge

18,000 lots.
Falling behind.

The City owns roughly 18,000 vacant lots that need regular grass cutting from late April through early November. Crews run every day — yet each lot is only reached on a 7-week cycle. By the time a crew returns, grass is long overgrown: a code problem, a blight problem, and a steady source of resident complaints.

03The Real Constraint

It isn't equipment sitting idle. It's labor.

All 24 tractor/cutter crews are staffed and running on a normal day. That full effort is exactly what produces today's seven-week cycle. So reaching four weeks is not a matter of using what we own more efficiently — it requires more cutting capacity on the street, and capacity means people. That is the problem this proposal solves without a proportional hiring spree.

05The Solution

Autonomous mowing,
supervised by your crew.

The Wright Autonomous Stander ZK 72" (Greenzie autonomy, RTK-GPS, obstacle detection, phone remote-stop) mows the open interior of a lot on its own while a worker trims edges and clears debris in parallel. One operator stays with the machine at all times — safe in the public right-of-way.

The unit is a labor multiplier, not a driverless robot. Field operators report it turns a three-person crew into a two-person crew at the same output.

07Why Autonomous, Not More Tractors

Same 18 crews.
Fewer people to hire.

Filling the gap with conventional crews means roughly 45 new hires — likely impossible under the labor constraint. Built as autonomous truck units — two mowers, two operators, one shared driver — the same 18 crews need about 27 staff.

The deeper point: mowing speed is not the bottleneck. A 72" deck clears a lot's interior in about a minute — the day is consumed by trimming, debris, and driving between lots. Autonomy plus GIS routing attacks exactly those costs.

09The Operating Brain

GIS route optimization.

Machines only hit a four-week cycle if no day is wasted driving. Our routing platform assigns every lot a fixed cut-slot on a repeating four-week schedule and sequences each crew's day into tight, contiguous clusters.

ZONE
