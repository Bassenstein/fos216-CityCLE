Gas Turbine Exhaust — Source Characterization

The GE 7HA.01 in simple cycle exhausts approximately 14 million lb/hr of combustion gases at ~1,100°F and atmospheric pressure at the turbine exit flange. This exhaust stream is the thermal source for all downstream heat recovery. Understanding its composition, temperature profile, and flow characteristics drives every sizing decision in the HRSG and distribution system.

Key design constraint
The HRSG must maintain stack exhaust temperature above ~190°F (acid dew point for NG combustion) to prevent sulfuric acid condensation on the economizer tubes. The 190°F stack design point in this document includes a 20°F margin above the calculated dew point. A condensing economizer could recover an additional 15–25 MW-th but requires acid-resistant materials (304L SS or Corten) and a drain neutralization system — budget accordingly if pursued.

Heat Recovery Steam Generator (HRSG)

The HRSG is a single-pressure, horizontal gas-flow, natural-circulation unit with selective catalytic reduction (SCR) and CO oxidation catalyst modules integrated in the gas path. Unfired in base configuration, with provision for supplemental duct firing up to 120 MW-th thermal input. All pressure parts per ASME Section I Power Boilers.

03

Absorption Chilling Integration

IP/LP steam at 50 psig / 300°F is extracted from the HRSG evaporator section and directed to double-effect lithium bromide (LiBr) absorption chillers. At a COP of 1.2–1.4, each MW-th of steam input produces 1.2–1.4 MW-th of cooling effect, eliminating the need for electric centrifugal chillers for 60% of the campus cooling load. The economic argument: steam is available at near-zero marginal fuel cost once the turbine is running.

How double-effect LiBr absorption works

Steam enters the high-temperature generator at ~150 psig and boils dilute LiBr solution, driving refrigerant vapor (water) upward. The refrigerant passes through a second-effect generator, condenser, then expansion valve to the evaporator where it absorbs heat from the chilled water loop at 44°F. The absorber closes the cycle, rejecting heat to the cooling tower. The net effect: heat in → cold out, with no compressor shaft power.

Efficiency advantage
Double-effect machines achieve COP 1.2–1.4 vs. single-effect COP 0.7. At $0 marginal steam cost vs. $0.06/kWh electric: a 1,500-TR absorption chiller saves ~$1.8M/year vs. an equivalent electric centrifugal chiller at Phase 1 IT load.

Process Steam Integration

Process steam from the LP ring (30 psig / 275°F) serves three parallel end uses via the internal campus distribution loop: building HVAC heat exchange, server hall humidification, and process / district energy taps. A single HP header feeds PRV station A (300→150 psig) for the absorption chillers, then PRV station B (150→30 psig) with desuperheat injection feeds the LP ring.

PRV station B desuperheat requirement
Reducing from 150 psig to 30 psig lowers saturation temperature from 366°F to 274°F but does not automatically desuperheat superheated steam. PRV station B must include a spray-type desuperheater (demineralized water injection) controlled by TIC-301 to ensure steam entering the LP ring is saturated — not superheated — at 274°F. Superheated steam at 30 psig entering humidifiers or heat exchangers causes control instability and can damage equipment.

Humidification load sizing — Cleveland climate basis

Cleveland's design winter condition is -5°F / 65% RH outdoor. When this air is heated to the data center supply temperature of ~72°F, relative humidity drops to ~3% without humidification — far below the ASHRAE A1-A2 class minimum of 20% RH and the target 45–55% for electrostatic discharge control. Steam required to humidify 100,000 CFM of outdoor air from 3% to 50% RH at 72°F supply is approximately 1,800 lb/hr — scaling with outdoor air fraction and IT load.

05

Campus Internal Steam Distribution Loop

The internal loop is a closed, three-pressure system: 300 psig HP header → 150 psig HP ring (absorption chillers) → 30 psig LP ring (building and process) → atmospheric condensate return. All three circuits are physically separate, thermodynamically coupled through the PRV cascade and the condensate receiver / deaerator. Design standard: ASME B31.1 Power Piping throughout.

Figure 5.1 — Campus steam loop — pressure tier structural diagram 3-tier cascade: HP header → HP ring → LP ring → condensate return

Condensate return system

Condensate returns from all steam-consuming equipment to a central atmospheric deaerator (DA-001) sized at 10,000 gallons with 20-minute retention time. The deaerator removes dissolved oxygen to <7 ppb via tray-type steam stripping, preventing oxygen-induced corrosion of the HRSG tubes and feedwater system. Feedwater pumps P-001A/B (duty/standby) return treated condensate at 600 psig to the HRSG economizer inlet.

⚠
Dissolved oxygen in feedwater is the leading cause of pitting corrosion in HRSG economizer and evaporator tubes. The deaerator must maintain effluent O₂ below 7 ppb at all loads. Add sodium sulfite O₂ scavenger injection downstream of the deaerator as a backup chemical treatment layer. Test DA-001 effluent weekly; respond to any reading above 20 ppb within 24 hours.

District Energy Spine

Available steam beyond campus consumption is exported to the Slavic Village Industrial Valley district energy spine at 50 psig / 300°F — the same IP/LP extraction pressure, without further pressure reduction. Customer-side PRV stations step down to end-use pressure at each building. Morabito would be the first district steam provider in Cleveland since CLECO / Dominion ceased operations in 2018.

Emissions Control System

Cuyahoga County is a USEPA ozone nonattainment area, triggering LAER (Lowest Achievable Emission Rate) rather than BACT for this project. The emission control chain achieves 2.0 ppm NOx at the stack — the Ohio RACT limit — through DLN combustors at the turbine followed by SCR with urea injection in the HRSG gas path.

Overall thermal efficiency — system-level summary
Simple cycle 7HA.01 efficiency: ~38–40%. Adding the HRSG and steam turbine (combined cycle): ~62%. Adding absorption chilling and process heat reuse: effective campus-level energy utilization rises to ~78–82% of fuel input energy delivered as useful work — electricity to grid, cooling to data center, heat to campus and district customers. This is the core value proposition of the integrated Morabito energy campus vs. a conventional data center drawing power from the grid.
