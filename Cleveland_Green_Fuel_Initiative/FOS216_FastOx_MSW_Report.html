01 — The Problem

Cleveland Is Paying More Every Year
To Bury Its Own Waste

Every year, the City of Cleveland pays a landfill operator to accept its Municipal Solid Waste — and every year, that bill grows. Tipping fees escalate with inflation, landfill capacity tightens, and Ohio EPA regulatory surcharges add another layer of cost on top. At the end of each contract cycle, the city owns nothing: no infrastructure, no energy, no asset. Just a receipt.

The Division of Waste Collection and Disposal has seen its total budget grow from $23.4 million in 2016 to nearly $39.6 million in 2024 — a 69% increase in eight years. The disposal contract line alone — what the city pays per ton to landfill residential MSW — swung from $4.2 million in 2016 to a peak of $10.8 million in 2021.

The question The FixOurStreets216 Project is posing to the City of Cleveland is direct: what if, instead of writing perpetually escalating checks to a landfill, Cleveland built an asset that converts its own waste into clean hydrogen and electricity — and owned the revenue?

⚠ The Escalation Risk

Midwest landfill tipping fees have averaged 3–4% annual growth, driven by tightening regional capacity and increasing regulatory burden. At that rate, Cleveland's annual disposal contract will exceed $13 million by 2035 — and the city will still have nothing to show for it.

03 — FastOx Technology

Sierra Energy FastOx
How It Works

FastOx is a pressurized gasification system developed by Sierra Energy. Unlike incineration — which burns waste to produce heat — FastOx converts waste into syngas (hydrogen and carbon monoxide) through a high-temperature chemical process in an oxygen-injected vessel. The key distinction: no combustion occurs in the gasifier itself. Municipal Solid Waste goes in; clean hydrogen, electricity, and an inert stone byproduct come out.

The system was piloted at Fort Hunter Liggett, California, processing military base MSW. It operates at temperatures exceeding 3,000°F, breaking down virtually all organic compounds — including hazardous materials — into elemental components.

ℹ City-Owned vs. Operator Model

This analysis models Cleveland as the owner and operator of the FastOx system — meaning the city bears full CAPEX but keeps 100% of energy revenues. An alternative structure — where Sierra Energy or a licensed operator owns the facility and charges Cleveland a below-market "gate fee" — would eliminate capital risk but also eliminate most upside. Both models result in lower per-ton disposal cost than current landfill contracts. This report focuses on city ownership because it produces the strongest long-term financial and strategic position.

💡 The Trajectory Argument

By year 20, the landfill path is still writing a check every year — and that check is larger than it was in 2019. The FastOx path has a massive upfront cost but a declining marginal cost as CAPEX is amortized. More importantly, after year 20 the FastOx system continues operating at O&M cost alone — roughly $24M/year — while generating ~$9M/year in energy revenue. The landfill path has no equivalent terminal value.

The IRA 45V Credit: A Game-Changer

The Inflation Reduction Act's Section 45V clean hydrogen production tax credit provides up to $3 per kilogram of hydrogen produced from qualifying clean processes for the first 10 years of operation. Waste-derived hydrogen from FastOx is a strong candidate for this credit, depending on lifecycle emissions accounting guidance the EPA is finalizing.

At 78 kg H₂/ton × $3/kg × 50% of Cleveland's 130,000-ton MSW stream × 10 years, the 45V credit alone would generate approximately $121.7 million in federal tax credits — reducing the net 20-year FastOx cost by that full amount and moving the crossover point approximately 2–3 years earlier.

06 — Adding Recycling

What Happens When You Add
Recycling to the Feedstock?

Cleveland processes approximately 10,000–20,000 tons of curbside recyclables per year through a separate contract with Rumpke. The city pays Rumpke to process the material and receives a modest per-ton revenue payment for uncontaminated recyclables. Adding the recycling stream to FastOx feedstock eliminates the Rumpke processing cost and adds tonnage — but also forfeits the recycling revenue and requires a slightly larger system.

⚠ Important Caveat on Recycling Feedstock

Routing all recyclables through FastOx destroys high-value materials (aluminum, clean cardboard) that fetch $1,500/ton or more in recycling markets — far more than their energy content value in FastOx. The optimal strategy is a hybrid model: clean, recoverable materials continue to the recycling market; contaminated recyclables and residual fractions (~8,000–12,000 t/yr) feed FastOx. The full 20,000-ton scenario modeled below is a ceiling, not a recommendation.

📊 Key Finding

Adding the full recycling stream to FastOx feedstock makes the system marginally more cost-competitive — primarily by eliminating the Rumpke processing contract. However, the incremental improvement is modest (~$3.6M over 20 years) compared to the much larger strategic value of recovering high-value recyclables through traditional markets. The strongest case for FastOx does not require routing all recyclables through the system.

07 — Conclusion

The Case for Action
What Cleveland Should Do Next

Bottom Line Assessment

FastOx is not a 7-year cost-savings play. Over a short window, continued landfill contracts are cheaper in absolute terms. But framing the decision this way is like refusing to build a water treatment plant because renting bottled water is cheaper this year. The landfill path is a permanent liability. The FastOx path is a 25-year infrastructure investment that generates clean energy, eliminates tipping fee exposure, and creates a municipal asset.

With full IRA Section 45V clean hydrogen credits applied, the 20-year FastOx premium over continued landfill contracts narrows to approximately $362 million — financed over 25 years, that is roughly $14.5 million per year in net additional cost. Cleveland currently spends that much on the Division of Waste in a single budget year. The question is not whether the city can afford this — it is whether Cleveland's leadership can adopt an infrastructure time horizon longer than the next election cycle.

The strategic case is even stronger than the financial one: energy independence (no exposure to landfill operator pricing power), decarbonization (waste-derived H₂ for city fleet vehicles or sale to industrial users), resilience (distributed municipal energy production), and economic development (construction jobs, permanent operations jobs, potential anchor for a hydrogen economy in the Industrial Valley).

Recommended Next Steps for the City

1. Commission an independent tonnage study. The city does not publicly disclose annual MSW tonnage. A precise tonnage figure is required to right-size any FastOx system and validate the financial projections in this report.

2. Engage Sierra Energy for a site-specific feasibility study. The Ridge Road Transfer Station is the logical anchor site — the city already owns the land and infrastructure. A Sierra Energy feasibility engagement would produce project-specific CAPEX, yield, and O&M figures rather than industry averages.

3. File for IRA 45V pre-qualification. The Department of Energy's clean hydrogen guidance is still evolving. Early engagement with the federal process positions Cleveland to maximize tax credit eligibility and signal commitment to hydrogen infrastructure investment.

4. Explore revenue bond financing. A 25-year municipal revenue bond backed by disposal savings and projected H₂ revenue could fund the CAPEX without general fund impact. The bond market has financed comparable waste-to-energy projects in other municipalities.

5. Model the hybrid recycling scenario. Rather than routing all recyclables to FastOx, a materials recovery facility (MRF) front-end — recovering clean metals, cardboard, and glass before the remainder feeds FastOx — maximizes total resource value from the waste stream.

✓ The FixOurStreets216 Position

The FixOurStreets216 Project urges the City of Cleveland to initiate a formal FastOx feasibility study as a first step — not a full commitment, but a data-gathering exercise that would answer the remaining questions before any capital decision is made. The cost of a feasibility study is negligible compared to the potential 25-year value of the technology. Cleveland has a window, right now, to become a model for mid-sized American cities on waste-derived clean energy. That window will not remain open indefinitely.
